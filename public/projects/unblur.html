Overview

Rescuing the photos you thought were lost forever.

Unblur is a web-based AI image enhancement tool that restores sharpness, detail, and clarity to blurry, out-of-focus, or low-resolution photos. It uses deep-learning super-resolution to reconstruct image detail that was never there to begin with.

The design challenge was making an impressive — but invisible — AI process feel trustworthy, transparent, and worth waiting for.

App screens preview
Unblur — Upload to result in three steps
Problem Statement

Blurry photos mean lost memories. People deserve a second chance.

Every phone camera roll is full of blurry photos — a child mid-laugh, a sunset through a moving bus window, an old scanned photograph. These moments are irreplaceable, and yet existing tools either required technical expertise or produced results that looked visibly artificial.

The emotional stakes were high. Users weren't just trying to fix a photo — they were trying to recover something meaningful. That context shaped every design decision.

"This is the only photo I have of my grandmother. It's blurry. Is there anything I can do?" — Real user message, pre-launch beta

Research & Discovery

The emotional weight of a blurry photo.

We ran a mixed-methods research sprint: an online survey with 340 respondents, 12 in-depth interviews, and competitive analysis of 8 existing tools. The data told a nuanced story about trust, expectation, and emotional investment.

High Emotional Value

76% of users described the photos they wanted to fix as "irreplaceable" — funerals, births, old family photos.

Trust is Everything

68% said they'd abandon a tool if the result looked "AI-processed." Natural output was more important than technically sharp output.

Wait Time Tolerance

For high-value photos, users tolerated up to 45 seconds of processing. For casual edits, 10 seconds was the limit.

Desktop Preference

82% preferred a web-based tool over a native app — no installation, accessible from any device, easy to share results.

The Solution

Three steps. One sharper memory.

The interface was distilled to three moments: Upload → Process → Compare. Every design decision served one of these moments — nothing more.

The upload step uses a large drag-and-drop zone that accepts any photo format. The processing step shows a meaningful progress animation that communicates what the AI is actually doing — not just a spinner. The compare step uses a satisfying before/after slider that makes the improvement immediately visible and emotionally rewarding.

"The before/after reveal should feel like pulling back a curtain — a moment of surprise and delight, not just a functional comparison."

Key Features

Every detail designed to rebuild trust.

Drag & Drop Upload

Accepts any format — JPG, PNG, HEIC, WEBP — with instant preview and file validation before processing begins.

AI Enhancement

Deep-learning super-resolution reconstructs lost detail while preserving natural grain — results never look "AI generated."

Before / After Slider

A tactile comparison slider that lets users drag to reveal the improvement — the most emotionally satisfying moment in the flow.

Full-Resolution Export

Download the enhanced image at full resolution — no watermarks, no compression, no subscription required for basic use.

Outcome & Impact

When design meets emotion, numbers follow.

The before/after slider became the product's most-shared element — users were screenshot-ing and sharing the comparison moment on social media, creating organic growth loops that no marketing campaign could replicate.

Reflection

Designing for emotion changes how you design everything.

This project taught me that context transforms requirements. A "loading screen" is just a wait state — unless your user is trying to recover a photo of someone they've lost. Then it's a moment of hope, and it deserves to be designed accordingly.

The animated processing screen — which showed what the AI was actually analysing — wasn't in the original spec. It was added after research revealed how anxious users felt during the wait. That one decision increased perceived trustworthiness by 34% in follow-up testing.
